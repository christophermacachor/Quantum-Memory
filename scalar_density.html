3.1 Scalars, Pseudoscalars, Vectors and Pseudovectors

In the ordinary quaternion theory we construct an object $q = c + x\mathbf{i}$ which essentially only distinguishes two types of objects: the scalars and the vectors. However, vectors split into two disjoint sets — the polar vectors and the axial vectors (arising from vector products of polar vectors; often called pseudovectors). In three dimensions we also have scalars and pseudoscalars.

These distinctions are well illustrated if we consider three vectors $\mathbf{g}$, $\mathbf{\Omega}$, $\boldsymbol{\xi}$ and their reflection in a mirror. If the real world has a right-handed system of axes, the mirror-world has a left-handed system. The vector $\boldsymbol{\xi}$ is reversed in direction, but the vector $\mathbf{g} \times \mathbf{\Omega}$ is unchanged.

A vector equation should never contain a polar vector and an axial vector — they are essentially independent entities. The same is true of scalars and pseudoscalars.

Coordinate Transformation

Under the general coordinate transformation (using the summation convention):

$$x'_i = a_{ij} x_j$$

If $\det(a_{ij}) = -1$ (reflection), and:

$$\mathbf{g} = (0,0,1) \quad \Rightarrow \quad \mathbf{g}' = (0,0,1)$$ $$\mathbf{\Omega} = (1,0,0) \quad \Rightarrow \quad \mathbf{\Omega}' = (1,0,0)$$ $$\boldsymbol{\xi} = (0,1,0) \quad \Rightarrow \quad \boldsymbol{\xi}' = (0,-1,0)$$

Using $(\mathbf{g} \times \mathbf{\Omega})'_i = \varepsilon_{ijk} a_{j\alpha} g_\alpha a_{k\beta} \Omega_\beta$, we find:

$$(\mathbf{g} \times \mathbf{\Omega})_i = (0,1,0) \quad \text{whilst} \quad (\mathbf{g} \times \mathbf{\Omega})'_i = (0,1,0)$$ The pseudovector is unchanged under reflection.

The Levi-Civita Tensor as Scalar Density

In three dimensions, the highest-order completely skew-symmetric tensor is proportional to $\varepsilon_{ijk}$. Let us call it $T_{ijk}$:

$$T_{ijk} = \pm \varepsilon_{ijk}$$ Positive sign for even permutations of $(1,2,3)$; negative for odd permutations.

Such a tensor has only one distinct component value, similar to a scalar. However, whereas a scalar is unchanged on rotating coordinate systems, this is not so for $T_{ijk}$. Consider the component $T'_{123}$:

$$T'_{123} = a_{1\alpha} a_{2\beta} a_{3\gamma} T_{\alpha\beta\gamma}$$ $$= a_{1\alpha} a_{2\beta} a_{3\gamma} \varepsilon_{\alpha\beta\gamma} T_{123}$$ $$= (\det a_{ij}) \varepsilon_{123} T_{123} = (\det a_{ij}) T_{123}$$

Scalar Densities and Tensor Densities

More generally, a single component $T$ transforming like:

$$T' = (\det A)^n T$$ is called a scalar density of weight $n$. Scalar densities of weight 1 are called pseudoscalars.

A tensor multiplied by a scalar density of weight $n$ becomes a tensor density of weight $n$. For example, the 1st-order tensor $w_i$ (vector) transforming as:

$$w'_i = a_{ij} w_j$$ is a zeroth-order tensor density (true vector).

Whereas the vector product $\varepsilon_{ijk} w_j v_k = p_i$ is a tensor density of weight 1:

$$p'_i = (\det A) \, a_{ij} p_j$$ This is called a pseudovector or bivector.

There is a direct relation between bivectors and skew-symmetric tensors of 2nd order $T_{ij}$.
