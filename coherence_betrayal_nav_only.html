I. The Accident That Changed Everything

On December 17, 1938, Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann bombarded uranium with neutrons in a Berlin laboratory. They expected to create heavier elements. Instead, they found barium — an element half the mass of uranium.

Lise Meitner and Otto Frisch, exiled in Sweden, interpreted the data over Christmas. The nucleus had split. Not gradually. Not gracefully. It had ruptured like a torn membrane, releasing energy that dwarfed any chemical reaction.

"One could almost see the atomic nucleus splitting like a water droplet — but a droplet that, upon dividing, released not surface tension, but the binding energy of creation itself."

Frisch named it fission — borrowing from biology, where cells divide to create life. The irony was cruel: this division created only death. The energy released was not merely thermal. It was the coherence tax — the price of breaking a fold in the implicate order.
